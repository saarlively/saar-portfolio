Usability Testing: AR Look and Feel

Usability study evaluating first-time experience, perceived quality, and early friction points for a Gen1 spatial computing prototype. The goal was to validate whether new users could complete key tasks independently and to identify the most critical experience issues to address before scaling.

UX Researcher Methods: Usability Testing · Semi-Structured Interviews · Card Sorting · Thematic Analysis Tools: Dovetail · Qualtrics · Respondent · OBS · Python
Overview

This study focused on how people perceived the product’s look and feel, and how effectively they could use it during an initial session. We combined hands-on usability tasks with qualitative interview questions to understand both performance and perception.

  • Assess first-time usability across core workflows
  • Identify friction points that reduce trust or perceived quality
  • Explore how users mentally position the product relative to the market (to support pricing and messaging decisions)

Study design

Participants completed a guided hands-on session with minimal assistance. The session combined structured tasks with follow-up interview questions to capture both usability performance and perception.

  • Hands-on tasks across core interactions and workflows
  • Semi-structured interview questions on comfort, trust, and perceived quality
  • Follow-up reflection on value, positioning, and adoption barriers

Key themes

Synthesis focused on experience signals that influence trust, comfort, and perceived product readiness. Findings were clustered into themes across physical interaction, early usability, and confidence in the system.

  • First-time clarity: where users expected more guidance or feedback
  • Perceived quality signals: moments that increased or reduced trust in the hardware
  • Comfort and fit: friction points that could impact extended use
  • Workflow continuity: how easily users could stay “in flow” during core tasks
Market framing & pricing perception

In addition to usability testing, we explored how users mentally positioned the product within the broader computing landscape.

We conducted a structured card sorting exercise to evaluate how participants categorized the device relative to existing technologies, including:

  • Productivity laptops
  • Immersive VR/XR headsets
  • Emerging hybrid computing devices

The goal was to understand category alignment, perceived functional equivalence, and implied pricing expectations — without explicitly anchoring participants to a specific price point. This activity provided insight into how users interpret the product’s value proposition within the competitive ecosystem.

Impact

The study informed design and product decisions by clarifying where the experience felt intuitive versus where additional guidance, refinement, or repositioning could improve first-time success and perceived readiness.

  • Mapped high-friction moments in early usage
  • Identified perception drivers that affect trust and confidence
  • Supported product positioning discussions through market framing research
